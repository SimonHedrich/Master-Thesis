# **Optimization and Deployment of Deep Learning Models for Real-Time Object Recognition on Resource-Constrained Embedded Hardware**

## **1\. Executive Summary**

The deployment of real-time object detection models on resource-constrained embedded edge hardware represents a highly complex intersection of computer vision architecture design, hardware-specific optimization, and domain-specific applied machine learning. Based on an exhaustive review of current literature, model architectures, and hardware profiling, achieving a target inference latency of ≤30ms at a 640×640 resolution on ARM Cortex-A76 class CPUs and the Qualcomm QCS605 (Hexagon 685 DSP) imposes severe architectural constraints. The absolute memory budget of ≤500MB further restricts operational parameters, mandating the utilization of highly optimized nano-scale convolutional networks or heavily quantized architectures, effectively ruling out massive foundation models for direct on-device inference.

The analysis indicates that three distinct model families stand out as the strongest candidates for this thesis. First, the latest iterations of the YOLO family—specifically YOLOv11n and YOLOv12n—offer unparalleled accuracy-to-parameter ratios and are natively supported by mature export pipelines, although they require aggressive quantization to meet latency targets on pure CPU architectures. Second, PicoDet emerges as an exceptionally strong candidate due to its mobile-first design, utilizing an Enhanced ShuffleNet (ESNet) backbone to achieve sub-10ms latencies on ARM CPUs natively. Finally, NanoDet-Plus provides an ultra-lightweight alternative, consuming less than 2MB of memory in FP16 format, making it structurally immune to the memory constraints of the embedded environment. To bridge the capacity gap inherent in these nano-scale models, particularly given the severe long-tail distribution and occlusion challenges characteristic of wildlife camera trap imagery, the application of Channel-Wise Knowledge Distillation (CWD) from a larger domain-specific teacher model (such as Google's SpeciesNet) is identified as a critical and highly recommended optimization pathway.

## **Detailed Research Survey**

To fully contextualize the recommendations and the subsequent comparative matrices, an exhaustive survey of the current state of real-time object detection, embedded deployment methodologies, knowledge distillation techniques, and the wildlife monitoring domain is required.

### **Part 1: Real-Time Object Detection Architectures**

The landscape of real-time object detection has been historically bifurcated into anchor-based and anchor-free Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), and more recently, hybrid CNN-Transformer architectures. Tracing the evolution of these models is essential to understanding their memory footprints, computational complexity, and suitability for the Qualcomm QCS605 and Raspberry Pi 5\.

The "You Only Look Once" (YOLO) lineage represents the definitive standard for real-time detection, evolving from rudimentary grid-based predictions to sophisticated anchor-free, attention-driven paradigms over the span of eight years.1 The foundational early iterations (YOLOv1 through YOLOv3), developed in the Darknet framework, utilized grid-based bounding box regression and predefined anchor boxes, which historically struggled with varying object scales and aspect ratios.1 The introduction of Cross Stage Partial Network (CSPNet) backbones in YOLOv4 and YOLOv5 significantly improved gradient flow and reduced parameter counts, establishing YOLOv5 as a long-standing staple for edge deployment due to its integration into robust export pipelines.2 Subsequent iterations pushed hardware optimization further; YOLOv6 and YOLOv7 introduced hardware-aware network design and Extended Efficient Layer Aggregation Networks (E-ELAN), which systematically optimized memory access costs on GPUs, though these optimizations did not always translate linearly to CPU or DSP architectures.2

A definitive architectural shift occurred with YOLOv8, which introduced a fully anchor-free detection head and a C2f (Cross-Stage Partial bottleneck with two convolutions) module. This C2f module replaced the older C3 block, structurally enhancing feature richness and gradient propagation without inflating computational burden.3 Furthermore, YOLOv8 decoupled the objectness, classification, and regression tasks, which empirically improved convergence and generalization across diverse datasets.3 YOLOv9 continued this trajectory by introducing Programmable Gradient Information (PGI) and the Generalized Efficient Layer Aggregation Network (GELAN) to prevent data loss during deep hierarchical feature extraction.4

The most profound shift for edge deployment occurred with YOLOv10, which revolutionized the inference pipeline by structurally eliminating the need for Non-Maximum Suppression (NMS). By utilizing a consistent dual-assignment strategy during the training phase, YOLOv10 yields deterministic, NMS-free predictions directly from the network head, thereby drastically reducing the end-to-end inference latency on constrained devices where NMS post-processing often constitutes a significant bottleneck.5 Building upon this, YOLOv11 refined the CNN backbone with C3k2 blocks and spatial feature pyramids, specifically targeting minimal memory footprints during inference and maintaining unparalleled lightweight options for constrained devices like the Raspberry Pi.6

Most recently, YOLOv12 integrated attention mechanisms while maintaining edge-friendly parameter counts. YOLOv12-N achieves an impressive 40.6% COCO mAP with only 2.6 million parameters, operating at 1.64ms on an NVIDIA T4 GPU, effectively outperforming both YOLOv10-N and YOLOv11-N while requiring fewer floating-point operations.8 However, comprehensive benchmarking reveals that on pure ARM CPUs like the Cortex-A76 found in the Raspberry Pi 5, even standard nano variants of the YOLO family struggle to break the 100ms barrier without extensive hardware-specific compilation or quantization, necessitating further compression strategies.10

Parallel to the evolution of CNNs, Detection Transformers (DETR) re-engineered object detection as a set prediction problem utilizing bipartite matching, eliminating hand-crafted components like anchor design and NMS. RT-DETR (Real-Time DETR) and its successor, RT-DETRv2, represent Baidu's successful attempts to make DETR architectures real-time capable by utilizing an efficient hybrid encoder and decoupled cross-attention modules.6 While RT-DETRv2 achieves formidable accuracy (up to 47.9% mAP for the ResNet-18 variant), the computational complexity of its self-attention mechanisms scales quadratically with spatial resolution. Comparative analysis shows that YOLOv12-S beats RT-DETR-R18 by running 42% faster with only 45% of the parameters.8 Furthermore, transformer-based models generally exhibit suboptimal performance on legacy ARM CPUs and 2018-era DSPs due to a lack of hardware-level instruction optimization for LayerNorm and multi-head attention mechanisms, rendering them highly problematic for the QCS605.

For devices strictly constrained to sub-500MB RAM and ARM-based CPUs, ultra-lightweight, mobile-first architectures frequently outperform downscaled general-purpose models. The NanoDet and NanoDet-Plus family relies on an anchor-free design inspired by Fully Convolutional One-Stage (FCOS) object detection. NanoDet-Plus utilizes a Generalized Focal Loss (GFL) and an Assign Guidance Module (AGM) to stabilize training, paired with a highly efficient ShuffleNetV2 backbone.12 The NanoDet-Plus-m variant achieves 30.4% mAP at a 416x416 resolution with an astonishingly low 1.17 million parameters. When exported via the NCNN framework, it achieves inference latencies of approximately 19.77ms natively on standard ARM Cortex-A76 cores, fulfilling the ≤30ms latency requirement out of the box without requiring specialized DSP acceleration.12 Crucially, the entire model payload occupies less than 2MB in FP16 format, leaving the vast majority of the 500MB memory budget available for the runtime and operating system overhead.12

Similarly, PicoDet, developed within the PaddlePaddle ecosystem, optimizes the feature extraction process through an Enhanced ShuffleNet (ESNet) backbone and a Cross-Stage Partial Spatial Pyramid Pooling (CSP-PAN) neck.13 PicoDet-S utilizes a mere 0.99 million parameters to achieve 30.6% mAP. By extending standard depthwise convolutions to a 5x5 kernel size, PicoDet expands its receptive field without inflating FLOPs, making it highly synergistic with ARM NEON SIMD instructions.13 Consequently, PicoDet-S operates at an exceptional 4.8ms on mobile ARM CPUs at a 320x320 resolution.14 The DAMO-YOLO architecture, developed by Alibaba, offers another alternative by utilizing Neural Architecture Search (NAS) to generate the TinyNAS backbone, alongside a built-in multi-stage knowledge distillation pipeline during training.5 The DAMO-YOLO-T variant achieves 41.8% mAP with 8.5 million parameters 16, though it demands significantly more memory and compute than NanoDet or PicoDet.

Finally, legacy architectures remain relevant due to their deep integration into established embedded toolchains. Google's EfficientDet family scales baseline models using a compound scaling coefficient that jointly optimizes network width, depth, and resolution, and introduces the Bi-directional Feature Pyramid Network (BiFPN) for efficient multiscale feature fusion.17 EfficientDet-Lite0 operates at 25.69% mAP with a 4.4MB size and is deeply integrated into the TensorFlow Lite ecosystem, making it a highly stable, albeit less accurate, baseline for embedded environments.17 MobileNet-SSD variants, pairing a MobileNet (V1/V2/V3) feature extractor with a Single Shot MultiBox Detector (SSD) head, remain one of the most widely deployed architectures in legacy systems.18 While highly energy-efficient and capable of rapid execution on the Raspberry Pi 19, their accuracy (often sub-25% mAP on COCO) is generally superseded by modern anchor-free methods.14 Foundational anchor-free models like FCOS and CenterNet reduce detection to center-point keypoint estimation, but their vanilla implementations typically rely on heavy backbones (such as ResNet-101), rendering them too computationally expensive for the QCS605 without substantial modification.13

### **Part 2: Embedded & Mobile Deployment**

Deploying deep learning models to the Qualcomm QCS605 and the Raspberry Pi 5 requires navigating a rigorous transformation pipeline. The strict constraint of ≤30ms per frame mandates the absolute utilization of hardware accelerators, specifically the Hexagon 685 DSP on the QCS605, and NEON SIMD vector instructions on the Cortex-A76.

The Raspberry Pi 5 serves as the designated development proxy. Its ARM Cortex-A76 cores support advanced NEON vector instructions and FP16 half-precision arithmetic. Empirical profiling indicates that utilizing the NCNN framework—an open-source high-performance neural network inference framework developed by Tencent that is deeply optimized for ARM SIMD—allows ultra-lightweight models like NanoDet and PicoDet to achieve sub-20ms latencies purely on the CPU.12 NCNN's memory allocation is strictly bounded and avoids dynamic memory allocation during inference, ensuring the ≤500MB constraint is safely and consistently met.12 Alternatively, the TensorFlow Lite (TFLite) ecosystem provides robust support for EfficientDet-Lite and YOLO models, leveraging the XNNPACK delegate to accelerate execution on the Raspberry Pi 5 ARM cores.17 While ONNX Runtime provides an ARM execution provider, it generally exhibits heavier RAM utilization overhead compared to NCNN or bare-metal TFLite implementations.

However, the ultimate target hardware is the Qualcomm QCS605 System-on-Chip (SoC), featuring an octa-core Kryo 300 CPU, an Adreno 615 GPU, and the Hexagon 685 DSP capable of approximately 2.1 TOPS.22 To leverage the dedicated matrix-multiplication acceleration of the Hexagon DSP, developers are required to utilize the Snapdragon Neural Processing Engine (SNPE) SDK.23 The operational workflow strictly mandates training the model in PyTorch or TensorFlow, exporting to an ONNX or TFLite intermediate representation, and utilizing the snpe-onnx-to-dlc or snpe-tflite-to-dlc compilation binaries to generate a proprietary Deep Learning Container (.dlc) file.23

To fit the memory bandwidth constraints and the integer math units of the Hexagon 685 DSP, the original FP32 network weights and activations must be quantized to INT8 precision. This conversion maps 32-bit floating-point values to 8-bit integers using the affine quantization formula $r = S(q - Z)$, where $r$ is the real FP32 value, $q$ is the quantized INT8 value, $S$ represents the scale factor, and $Z$ is the integer zero-point.

The SNPE toolchain natively supports Post-Training Quantization (PTQ) via the snpe-dlc-quantize utility, requiring the injection of an input list of representative calibration images to analyze activation histograms and determine optimal clipping ranges.24 While highly convenient, PTQ on modern anchor-free detectors frequently induces catastrophic accuracy degradation. Models like YOLOv11 or PicoDet rely on decoupled regression heads that output unbounded, precise coordinate offsets; these continuous representations are highly sensitive to the clipping and rounding errors introduced by 8-bit quantization, often resulting in mAP drops of 2% to 5%.

To mitigate the precision loss inherent in PTQ, Quantization-Aware Training (QAT) is heavily recommended. QAT integrates simulated quantization noise (fake quantization nodes) during the forward pass of the training phase, allowing the network weights to dynamically adjust and recover from the restricted INT8 bit-width via gradient descent. Given the small object sizes and low contrast typical in camera trap imagery, executing QAT prior to SNPE export is considered a critical requirement to preserve bounding box regression fidelity when executing on the Hexagon DSP. When executed correctly, the SNPE runtime parses the .dlc file, maps memory directly to the DSP using Qualcomm's FastRPC mechanism, and executes the graph. It must be noted that any unsupported operations within the model architecture will trigger a fallback to the Adreno 615 GPU or Kryo CPU, inducing severe context-switching latency penalties.23 Therefore, ensuring the chosen architecture consists entirely of SNPE-supported operations is paramount.

### **Part 3: Knowledge Distillation for Object Detection**

To fulfill the dual requirements of high mean Average Precision (mAP) and low inference latency, a robust strategy is to employ a heavily parameterized teacher model to supervise a lightweight, edge-compatible student model via Knowledge Distillation (KD). Unlike standard image classification KD, which simply aligns output probability distributions, object detection KD must effectively transfer both spatial localization knowledge and classification logits across dense, multi-scale feature maps.21

The literature delineates three primary distillation paradigms for object detection. Feature-Level Distillation forces the student model to match the intermediate spatial feature maps of the teacher, which is highly effective for dense prediction tasks as it explicitly transfers the learned spatial hierarchies, receptive fields, and texture sensitivities. Logit/Response-Level Distillation seeks to match the final prediction outputs (bounding boxes and class probabilities), while Relation-Based Distillation transfers the structural interactions and correlations between different feature maps or object queries rather than just the isolated feature magnitudes.27

A prominent and highly successful technique in recent literature is Channel-wise Knowledge Distillation (CWD). CWD addresses the capacity gap issue, wherein forcing a low-capacity student to strictly mimic the raw feature magnitudes of a high-capacity teacher causes optimization divergence and convergence failure. Instead, CWD normalizes the feature maps of both the teacher and student across the spatial dimensions for each independent channel using a Softmax function, mathematically converting the feature activations into spatial probability distributions.28 The distillation loss is then computed using the asymmetric Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between these corresponding distributions. CWD has demonstrated significant success in dense predictors; for example, applying CWD from a RetinaNet-R101 teacher to a RetinaNet-R50 student improves the baseline mAP while maintaining the smaller architecture's inference speed, effectively achieving a 2.5x compression ratio without catastrophic precision loss.30

Another advanced methodology is Focal and Global Distillation (FGD). FGD explicitly addresses the severe imbalance between foreground (objects of interest) and background (empty space) inherent in object detection feature maps. It utilizes a focal distillation module that forces the student to mimic the teacher's features exclusively on critical foreground pixels using computationally generated spatial and channel attention masks. This is combined with a global distillation module designed to capture broader contextual relationships between different pixels.21

Furthermore, Localization Distillation (LD) tackles the specific difficulty of distilling bounding box regression coordinates. Because bounding box regression is traditionally framed as predicting a Dirac delta distribution (a single deterministic numerical coordinate), standard distillation loss functions fail to capture the teacher's spatial uncertainty. LD reformulates bounding box regression into a probability distribution over a continuous range of locations utilizing Distribution Focal Loss (DFL). By doing so, the student explicitly learns the teacher's localization uncertainty, which dramatically improves the localization precision of small or highly occluded objects.31

Within the YOLO ecosystem, recent implementations of KD leverage a sophisticated combination of feature and logit distillation. For instance, utilizing a YOLOv8s model (11.2M parameters) as a teacher for a YOLOv8n model (3.0M parameters) yields a measured 1.18% absolute improvement in mAP, while operating with roughly 60% fewer parameters than the teacher model.32 The mathematical process involves modifying the student's holistic loss function to incorporate the distillation penalty: $Loss = \lambda_{task} L_{task} + \lambda_{KD} L_{KL}(T_i, S_i)$, smoothly bridging the gap between high accuracy and edge-compatibility. Similar pipelines exist for YOLOv9, where utilizing YOLOv9-e to supervise YOLOv9-c recaptures approximately 2.0% mAP that would otherwise be lost to the architecture compression.4

### **Part 4: Wildlife & Camera Trap Domain**

The deployment of real-time object detection models to physical camera traps introduces severe, domain-specific environmental and statistical hurdles that fundamentally diverge from standard benchmark datasets like COCO.

First, motion-triggered sensors frequently yield false positives due to wind, moving vegetation, or thermal shadows, resulting in massive volumes of Empty Frames. Datasets such as Snapshot Serengeti—which contains 7.1 million images across 2.65 million sequences—report that up to 76% of captured images are entirely devoid of animals.34 A continuous inference system operating on edge hardware must possess the capability to rapidly and aggressively filter these empty frames to conserve battery life, thermal thresholds, and memory bandwidth.

Second, wildlife ecosystems inherently generate Long-Tail Distributions. Class imbalances are extreme; common species may be represented by millions of images, whereas rare or elusive species may have fewer than fifty annotated examples globally. Models trained purely on global distributions struggle severely to maintain precision on the long tail of rare species. Third, systems must handle severe Illumination and Modality Shifts. Camera traps alternate dynamically between high-resolution RGB daylight images and low-resolution, low-contrast Infrared (IR) night imagery. Models trained exclusively on daylight RGB features experience catastrophic failure when inferring on IR data.35 Finally, mammals are frequently captured at extreme distances or obscured by dense foliage (Occlusion and Scale), representing a minuscule fraction of the 640x640 input resolution and compounding the difficulty of spatial localization.

To address these highly specific challenges, the research community has developed massive, specialized foundation models for the biological domain. While their parameter counts strictly prohibit direct execution on the QCS605 or Raspberry Pi 5, they represent ideal starting points for Transfer Learning or as highly capable Teacher models in a Knowledge Distillation pipeline.

Google has developed SpeciesNet, an image classifier engineered explicitly to accelerate camera trap analysis. SpeciesNet is trained on a massive, geographically diverse dataset of over 65 million camera trap images.36 Utilizing an EfficientNet V2 M architecture, it is capable of identifying over 2,000 specific labels, including diverse animal species, higher-level taxonomic groups (e.g., "mammalia"), and crucially, non-animal classes like "blank" and "vehicle".36 It typically operates as part of an ensemble alongside MegaDetector—a robust, class-agnostic object detector that first locates and crops animals or humans from the frame.36 The ensemble utilizes geofencing rules and taxonomic aggregation heuristics to improve reliability. SpeciesNet exhibits an 83% species-level identification accuracy and a remarkable 98.6% empty image exclusion accuracy.38 Because the architecture has been open-sourced under the Apache 2.0 license, its pre-trained weights can be directly leveraged for transfer learning.36

Furthermore, the BioCLIP and BioCLIP 2 models represent state-of-the-art vision-language foundation models designed specifically for general organismal biology. BioCLIP utilizes the CLIP (Contrastive Language-Image Pretraining) architecture, while BioCLIP 2 scales this methodology using a massive ViT-L/14 transformer architecture trained on the unprecedented TreeOfLife-200M dataset (comprising 214 million images spanning 952,000 taxonomic classes).41 Utilizing hierarchical contrastive learning, BioCLIP 2 demonstrates profound emergent properties: it naturally aligns inter-species ecological characteristics (e.g., separating freshwater from saltwater species without explicit habitat supervision) and separates intra-species variations.42 It improves zero-shot species classification by 18.1% over its predecessor.42 While its Vision Transformer architecture strictly violates the embedded hardware constraints of this thesis, its robust embedding space provides a profound mechanism for zero-shot labeling of unannotated regional data or serving as a teacher for feature-level distillation.

A robust transfer learning strategy for the student models requires high-quality, domain-specific annotated datasets. Snapshot Serengeti provides 7.1 million images with approximately 150,000 bounding box annotations across 61 African savanna categories.34 The iWildCam 2021 competition dataset provides a globally distributed dataset of over 200,000 training images explicitly targeting the complex problem of animal counting across sparse temporal bursts.43 Additionally, the iNaturalist 2021 dataset offers approximately 2.7 million images spanning 10,000 highly diverse species, serving as a foundational pillar for transferring general biological features to specific edge detectors.43

## **5\. Recommended Shortlist with Rationale**

To successfully achieve real-time object recognition (≤30ms at 640x640) under the strict RAM (≤500MB) and architectural constraints of the Qualcomm QCS605 and Raspberry Pi 5, the following models are shortlisted for deployment within the thesis:

### **YOLOv11n / YOLOv12n**

* **Suitability:** The Ultralytics ecosystem provides a flawless, production-ready ML pipeline for exporting to ONNX, TFLite, and NCNN. The architecture is highly optimized for edge deployment, natively supporting INT8 PTQ. YOLOv12n pushes the accuracy-latency pareto frontier to new heights, delivering 40.6% mAP with minimal parameters.6  
* **Key Weaknesses:** Despite being designated as "nano" variants, these models still utilize millions of parameters. Relying purely on the CPU of the Raspberry Pi 5 may yield latencies exceeding 40ms, making Hexagon DSP acceleration via SNPE strictly necessary. Furthermore, their anchor-free decoupled heads are highly susceptible to catastrophic precision drops during standard PTQ.  
* **Verification Required:** The thesis must empirically measure the mAP degradation of YOLOv11n/v12n when quantized to INT8 using Qualcomm's snpe-dlc-quantize. Quantization-Aware Training (QAT) may need to be custom-integrated into the Ultralytics training loop to recover precision.

### **PicoDet-S**

* **Suitability:** Designed explicitly for mobile ARM CPUs. With under 1 million parameters, it achieves 30.6% COCO mAP and operates at sub-10ms latencies natively on ARM Cortex cores. It cleanly beats the 30ms latency constraint even without DSP acceleration, thanks to its 5x5 depthwise convolutions and ESNet backbone.13  
* **Key Weaknesses:** Developed strictly within the PaddlePaddle ecosystem (PaddleDetection). This introduces significant friction when attempting to convert models to intermediate formats (ONNX/TFLite) and subsequently to the SNPE .dlc format compared to PyTorch-native models.  
* **Verification Required:** The conversion fidelity from the PaddleDetection framework to ONNX, and then to the SNPE .dlc format, must be validated. It must be ensured that the ESNet backbone and CSP-PAN neck are fully supported by Qualcomm's Hexagon DSP operational libraries.

### **NanoDet-Plus-m**

* **Suitability:** Operating with a mere 1.17M parameters and boasting a sub-2MB FP16 file size, this model requires virtually no RAM overhead, easily satisfying the 500MB constraint. Its native integration with the NCNN framework guarantees peak performance on the Raspberry Pi 5 proxy device.12  
* **Key Weaknesses:** The aggressive minimization of parameters severely limits its capacity to learn the long-tail distribution of hundreds of wildlife species. It may struggle significantly with the extreme morphological variations and occlusions present in the camera trap domain without heavy Knowledge Distillation.  
* **Verification Required:** The effectiveness of applying Channel-Wise Distillation (CWD) to transfer dense feature representations from a larger model (e.g., SpeciesNet or YOLOv8s) into NanoDet to mechanically compensate for its low parameter capacity.

### **Transfer Learning Pipeline: MegaDetector → SpeciesNet (Modified)**

* **Suitability:** While not a single end-to-end detector, utilizing a highly quantized MegaDetector to yield generic animal bounding boxes, sequentially followed by an ultra-lightweight MobileNetV3 classifier trained via knowledge distillation from SpeciesNet, mirrors the current industry standard for this domain.36  
* **Key Weaknesses:** A two-stage sequential pipeline introduces severe cascading latencies. The intermediate bounding box cropping and memory transfer between the detector runtime and the classifier runtime may easily breach the 30ms limit on the QCS605.  
* **Verification Required:** Benchmarking the absolute end-to-end latency of a cascaded NCNN or TFLite execution on the Raspberry Pi 5, specifically profiling the memory allocation overhead between the detection and classification stages.

## **6\. Open Questions & Gaps**

The literature survey reveals several critical gaps and technical contradictions that the thesis must directly address through empirical research:

1. **Quantization Sensitivity of Anchor-Free Heads in the Wildlife Domain:** While PTQ is extensively documented for standard, well-lit benchmark datasets like COCO, the literature lacks comprehensive studies on how INT8 quantization directly affects the continuous bounding box regression of modern anchor-free models (like YOLOv11 or PicoDet) specifically on small, heavily occluded mammals in low-contrast infrared imagery. It remains unknown if the loss of FP32 precision disproportionately destroys recall for distant, cryptic species.  
2. **SNPE Support for Modern Attention Mechanisms:** YOLOv12n achieves remarkable theoretical performance through the integration of attention mechanisms.8 However, it remains entirely unverified whether the 2018-era Hexagon 685 DSP compiler within the SNPE toolchain natively supports these specific mathematical attention blocks. If unsupported, SNPE will default them to the Kryo CPU, instantly destroying the latency budget through severe context-switching overhead.  
3. **Cross-Architecture Distillation Limits:** Current literature heavily documents distilling within identical architectural families (e.g., YOLOv8s acting as a teacher for YOLOv8n).32 There is a pronounced gap in methodologies detailing how to distill the rich, domain-specific taxonomic representations from a pure global classifier (like Google's SpeciesNet) or a massive vision-language transformer (like BioCLIP 2\) directly into the dense prediction heads of a single-stage anchor-free student detector (like NanoDet).  
4. **Hardware Proxy Fidelity:** While the Raspberry Pi 5 (ARM Cortex-A76) is an excellent mathematical proxy for the Kryo 300 CPU on the QCS605 22, its lack of a dedicated DSP means it cannot accurately predict DSP-accelerated INT8 performance. The thesis must establish a rigorous conversion baseline or theoretical FLOP-to-TOPS mapping to ensure Raspberry Pi benchmarks are legitimately predictive of final QCS605 deployment success.
